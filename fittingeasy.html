Problem

For people in wheelchairs, shopping clothes in a physical store is not always a pleasant experience. Despite they have the same needs to visit a phisical store as general people, they are facing more issues because of the big, heavy, and inconvenient wheelchairs, especially in terms of the following aspects:

  • Aisles between racks are too narrow for them to get through.
  • Racks and displays are too high to reach and sort through.
  • Accessible fitting rooms are often unusable because of being used for stock or other reasons.

Solution

Here we present FittingEasy, a convenient mobile application that helps reserve fitting rooms for both people in wheelchairs and general people. In addition, the application helps to pre-order clothes before getting to the physical store, and check the fit of the clothes by scanning the barcode on the clothes tag.

Process

This project followed an evidence-based design process where feedback was collected in every step of the way.

Research

To learn more about the problem space and our users, we interviewed several experts within the field including two wheelchair users, an occupational therapist, and a research scientise who specilizes in accessibility. We also launched a survey to collect both qualitative and quantitative data from real users. Finally, we conducted literature review to learn about existing products.

Through thorough research, we were able to determine following design criteria to guide our design process and final evaluation.

  • Learnability: How easy it is to learn for novice users.
  • Efficiency: How efficient it is for users to perform specific tasks.
  • Error: How many errors users make when performing tasks, and how server they are.
  • Satisfication: How satisfied users feel about it.

Interview

Through interviews, we acquired many key user insights and were able to identify our problem space.

"Clothes shops cramming too much stock in which leaves little room to get by in a wheelchair."

"The shelves are always so high, and I can never reach them!"

"Fitting rooms are the worst because they always let normies go in them, and I have to wait."

"About 1 in 10 assistants will ask if I want help or try and pack my clothes into my bag for me — DON'T EVER force your help on me!"

Survey

From our survey results, nearly 75% of respondents said they would go out for shopping. When comparing online clothes shopping and physical store shopping, 30.8% prefer shopping online, 15.4% prefer physical stores, and 53.8% have no preference between the two. People said they like the experience of physical stores, however, the physical store shopping experience can be very exhausting. When asked whether they would ask for assistance when shopping in stores, 46.2% respondents said they prefer not asking for any help.

Literature Review

Many solutions have been proposed and implemented to ease the inconvience of shopping for wheelchair users. Researchers at Pompeu Fabra University in Barcelona designed an app that applied augmented-reality technology to help them reach items in a high shelf. In addition, many high-tech companies have explored the concept of virtual fitting rooms as a potential solution. The customer can see themselves on the screen with a 3D copy of clothes.

Evaluation

Once we finished the prototype it was time to see how they performed with people. We recruited several experts and users to go through our prototype. Through cognitive walkthrough and user testing, we were able to get their impressions and identify issues on our design.

Participants

2 experts for cognitive walkthrough, and 4 users for user testing

Evaluation Methods

Cognitive walkthrough and user testing

Metrics Measured

Learnability, efficiency, error and satisfication

We set up several tasks in terms of core functions of our design for both experts and users to perform.

  • A novice user needs to sign up first and enter their body measurements.
  • User wants to reserve a fitting room.
  • User wants to scan an item and send it to the reserved fitting room, and wait until the fitting room is ready.

Cognitive Walkthrough

We conducted two cognitive walkthrough sessions with experts. The experts were given the same tasks that were designed for the users. The expert would identify issues as they tried to perform tasks, and also answered several questions to evaluate the interface.

After cognitive walkthrough, we made some changes on our design, and then performed user testing.

User Testing

We recruited 4 users to help us perform user testing. As they performed tasks, they were asked to think aloud. Once the user completed their tasks, several follow-up questions were asked. Then they rated their opinions of our design through the questionnaire including SUS.

Recommendations

From our testing and user feedback, we were able to identify issues existing in our design and provide the following recommendations for future work:

  • Cut off the unnecessary parts of measurements to reduce the burden of users, conduct research about what body measurements are necessary in terms of clothes.
  • Use dynamic images (e.g., GIF) as the measurement instruction images instead of static images, this way is more clear to users.
  • There should be a measurement service set up in store, offer assistance and tools for users to measure their body.
  • Adjust timeslots for reserving fitting room, conduct research about average time that users spend in the fitting room.
  • Our design should be included as a major function in online shopping, users can choose items and reserve a fitting room at home, and then go to the physical store to try on.
  • Add a call for assistance button in the app, it will be easier if they need help when in the fitting room.
  • Add instructions to help novice users get familiar with our app, let them know how our app works.

Conclusion

This is the first project that I worked from the very beginning to the end. I learned a lot about evidence-based design process. Being proactive in every step gives me a strong foundation for future design work.

Another thing that impressed me a lot when working for this project is how important accessible design or universal design is for people with impairments. One of my participants said: "You probably never think about what technology means to disabled people. For you, it is just an alternative option, but for us, it helps us to survive in this world." I believe our work has important impact on people, and we will keep working on it. I really hope more and more people can put their attention to special groups, because they are also a part of this world.
