Problem

People in wheelchairs are facing more issues when shopping in a physical store because of their big, heavy, and inconvenient wheelchirs, especially in terms of the following aspects. This project will focus on the last aspect.

  • Aisles between racks are too narrow for them to get through.
  • Racks and displays are too high to reach and sort through.
  • Accessible fitting rooms are often unusable because of being used for stock or other reasons.

Solution

Here we present FittingEasy, a convenient mobile application that helps people in wheelchairs smoothly go through try-on process. The main functions are as shown below.

  • Reserve fitting room online.
  • Pre-order clothes to try on before getting to the physical store.
  • Scan barcode on the clothes tag to check if it fits.

Process

This project followed an evidence-based design process where feedback was collected in every step of the way.

Research

To learn more about our problem space and user needs, we interviewed several experts within the field. We also launched a survey to collect both qualitative and quantitative data from users. And we conducted literature review to learn about existing products.

Through thorough research, we identified three major problems they are facing when shopping in a physical store. Compared with the other two, the last problem has more impact on them.

  • Aisles between racks are too narrow for them to get through.
  • Racks and displays are too high to reach and sort through.
  • Accessible fitting rooms are often unusable because of being used for stock or other reasons.

We also determined the following design criteria to guide our design process and final evaluation.

  • Learnability: How easy it is to learn for novice users.
  • Efficiency: How efficient it is for users to perform specific tasks.
  • Error: How many errors users make when performing tasks, and how severe they are.
  • Satisfication: How satisfied users feel about it.

Interview

We interviewed 4 participants including two wheelchair users, an occupational therapist, and a research scientist who specilizes in accessibility. Through interviews, we acquired many key user insights and were able to identify our problem space.

"Clothes shops cram too much stock in which leaves little room to get by."

"The shelves are always so high, and I can never reach them!"

"Fitting rooms are the worst because they always let normies go in them, and I have to wait."

"About 1 in 10 assistants will ask if I want help or try and pack my clothes into my bag for me — DON'T EVER force your help on me!"

Survey

We launched a survey that contains questions about their current shopping situation and their opinions about it. According to our survey results, we found some strong evidence to prove that people in wheelchairs are not able to enjoy physical store shopping experience, although they have the same need as we do.

  • Nearly 75% of respondents said they would go out for shopping.
  • Comparing online shopping and physical store shopping, 30.8% prefer shopping online, 15.4% prefer physical stores, and 53.8% have no preference between the two.
  • When asking whether they would ask for assistance when shopping in stores, 46.2% respondents said they prefer not asking for any help.

User Feedback

After we finished paper sketch and storyboards for our three design concepts, we launched a survey and interviewed experts to gather user feedback. Through this way, we got many valuable insights and reconsidered how we could better design our final solution. After group discussion, we made some changes about our design concepts, combining parts of them. Eventually, we had our final version of design concept.

Prototype

After determining our final design concept, we built wireframes to specify our design. And eventually we designed the prototype FittingEasy. It mainly contains the following steps.

  • Sign up and enter measurements. Users can change their measurements after in the profile page.
  • Reserve a fitting room. Users can check reservation details in the home page.
  • Scan barcode to check if the item fits. This featured function can help users reduce the times to try on.
  • Wait the fitting room to be prepared. FittingEasy will provide details about preparation process. Users can also check the remaining time.

Evaluation

After we finished the prototype, we recruited several experts and users to go through our application. Through cognitive walkthrough and user testing, we were able to get their impressions and identify issues on our design.

Participants

2 experts for cognitive walkthrough, and 4 users for user testing

Evaluation Methods

Cognitive walkthrough and user testing

Metrics Measured

Learnability, efficiency, error and satisfication

The graphs below are an overall evaluation on our design.

Cognitive Walkthrough

We conducted 2 cognitive walkthrough sessions with experts. The experts were given the same tasks that were designed for the users. The expert would identify issues as they tried to perform tasks, and also answered several questions about the interface.

User Testing

We recruited 4 users to help us perform user testing. As they performed tasks, we asked them to think aloud. Once the user completed their tasks, we asked several follow-up questions. Then they rated their opinions of our design through the questionnaire including SUS.

Recommendations

Based on testing and user feedback, we identified some issues existing in our design and provided the following recommendations for future work:

  • Cut off the unnecessary parts of measurements to reduce the burden of users, conduct research about what body measurements are necessary in terms of clothes.
  • Use dynamic images (e.g., GIF) as the measurement instruction images instead of static images, users would more clearly understand how to measure their body.
  • There should be a measurement service set up in store, offering assistance and tools for users to measure their body.
  • Adjust timeslots for reserving fitting room, conduct research about average time that users spend in the fitting room.
  • Our design should be included as a major function in online shopping, users can choose items and reserve a fitting room at home, and then go to the physical store to try on.
  • Add a "call for assistance" button in the app, it will be easier when they need help in the fitting room.
  • Add instructions to help novice users get familiar with our app, let them know how our app works.

Conclusion

This is the first project that I was involved from the very beginning to the end. I learned a lot about evidence-based design process. Being proactive in every step gives me a strong foundation for future design work.

Another thing that impressed me when working for this project is how important accessible design or universal design is for people with impairments. One of my participants said:

"You probably never think about what technology means to disabled people. For you, it is just an alternative option, but for us, it helps us to survive in this world."

I believe our work has important impact on people, and we will keep working on it. I really hope more and more people can put their focus on people who have special needs, because they are also a part of this world.
