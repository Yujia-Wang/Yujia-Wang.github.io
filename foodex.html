OVERVIEW

PROBLEM

Overseas Chinese students often live far from home and usually face a lot of pressure from college and studying. This can make finding nutritious meals every day a major problem. The obvious solution is to use food delivery services, which are quick and convenient. For overseas Chinese students, there are two main types of food delivery services they usually use, but they both come with some issues:

For traditional food delivery apps,

  • The total cost can be quite high.
  • It can be hard for students to communicate with drivers regarding navigation.

For the WeChat platform,

  • It has a critical mass but the whole service is unprofessional.

From the perspective of restaurants, they have expectations regarding food delivery services as well, including:

  • Improving sales
  • Facilitating communication with customers
  • Making the food delivery services more efficient

Thus, a design challenge emerged:

How can the affordability and communication of food delivery services be improved while maintaining the profit of restaurants?

SOLUTION

FoodEx is a WeChat Mini Program designed to improve affordability and communication of food delivery services for overseas Chinese students whilst maintaining the profit of restaurants. The main features are:

  • It’s WeChat-based - very familiar to target users and requires few set-up.
  • Foodpool - enables users to group-order food and get various discounts based on head counts.
  • Pre-organized pick-up station and fixed time - enables users to pick up food at pre-organised pick-up lockers.

From the perspective of restaurants,

  • Again, it’s WeChat-based - more professional, provides a lot of potential customers, and it’s easy to advertise.
  • Foodpool - social features facilitate sharing and advertising which improves sales.
  • Pre-organized pick-up station and fixed time – reduces communication efforts.

PROCESS

RESEARCH

We started our project by focusing on how overseas Chinese students eat every day, which we did for the following reasons:

  • They are more likely to deal with trivia on their own.
  • They are likely to face time constraints because of personal and academic activities, and thus would like to spend less energy on tasks such as cooking and food preparation.
  • They are likely having difficulties in facing a new dining culture.

Therefore, we conducted a survey to better understand how they overcome these difficulties.

SURVEY

We launched a survey with questions about their common dining options and opinions about them.

  • Nearly 75% of respondents said they would go out for shopping.
  • Comparing online shopping and physical store shopping, 30.8% prefer shopping online, 15.4% prefer physical stores, and 53.8% have no preference between the two.
  • When asked whether they would ask for assistance when shopping in stores, 46.2% respondents said they prefer not asking for any help.

According to our survey results, a lot of students are tired of on-campus dining options and are seeking alternatives. A popular and convenient solution would be using a food delivery service. However, they complained that they’ve had negative experiences with these services including Uber Eats, which is why we believe there is a design opportunity and why we decided to focus on this area.

Since food delivery services are a common choice among overseas students, we wanted to investigate the popular food delivery solutions/services existing on the market, and their using experience. We conducted several semi-structured interviews with overseas students for comparitive analysis:

DESIGN

Based on our research, we found that although WeChat is not a professional food delivery platform, many overseas Chinese students still prefer to order food from it. We believe there is a design opportunity to build a WeChat Mini-program that incorporates relevant food delivery features while utilizing the advantage of the critical mass of WeChat.

BRAINSTORMING

Since we have decided to focus on WeChat, we conducted a brainstorming session to come up with ideas around traditional food delivery apps to tackle these two major problems.

At the end of the brainstorming session, we came up with 20 ideas surrrounding these two issues. After considering their feasibility and discarding some of them, we narrowed it down to three ideas for each issue. We then sketched each idea and delivered them to users for feedback.

DESIGN ALTERNATIVES

Concept 1

The store sets up body scanning technology which would scan a customer's body and collect body size information. The information will directly send to customer's phone while the store won't have access to it. When the customer finds some favored clothes, they can use the phone to scan the price tag, and the phone will show the information about how this clothes fits. Also, the customer can check the virtual image of these clothes shown on a model.

Concept 2

Customers can reserce a fitting room before they go to the store. They can pre-order clothes online, or select clothes in stores depending on their preference. The selected clothes will be sent to the reserved fitting room before the customer arrives there. If the aisles are too narrow to get through, they can pre-order online and go straight to the fitting room without going through every aisle.

Concept 3

Users can browse clothes options via a large in-store interactive display screen. Users browse and search through a system that is similar to an online shopping experience, narrowing down categories and finding specific types of items. Clothes are displayed on the screen in actual size with relevant specification information, such as size, price, and color options. Users then have the option to have the clothes pulled for them, or sent directly to a specified fitting room to try on.

USER FEEDBACK

Eventually, we built our final version of design concept incorporating shining points of each original ones.

EVALUATION

After we finished the prototype, we had several experts and users go through our application. Through usability testing, we got their impressions and identified design flaws.

USABILITY TESTING

First, we developed 1 example task to help users familiarize with the think-aloud process, and 4 tasks that cover the main features of our app. They are:

  • Example Task: Enter measurements.
  • Task 1: Make a fitting room reservation and check the reservation detail.
  • Task 2: Pre-order the BDG Corduroy Trucker Jacket and make sure it has been successfully ordered.
  • Task 3: Scan a barcode of an item and send it to the fitting room.
  • Task 4: Imagine you are in the fitting room, and you want to try on more clothes. Order more clothes to try on.

4 users helped us perform usability testing. As they performed tasks, we asked them to think aloud. Once the users completed their tasks, we asked several follow-up questions. Then they rated our design through the questionnaire based on SUS. The SUS result is 84.375, indicating that users are very satisfied.
