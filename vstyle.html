USER GOALS

Based on the understanding of our dataset and users, we established two outlined goals with several specific sub-goals that our design should help users achieve:

Understanding how clothing styles change over time and vary around the world

  • Identifying trends over time - Fashion changes quickly and constantly. Identifying those changes over time may help users understand how and why these changes occur.
  • Identifying similarities and differences of cities - Every culture has specific influences on the population, and this also applies to each city. How people are dressing in a city represents the location and the personality of the area as well.

Discovering inspirations for dressing up

  • Identifying popular styles of clothing combinations - Besides the culture exploration of our design, a more practical usage could be assisting users and inspiring how they match clothing styles.

DESIGN

During the design phase, we encountered many challenges regarding how we can best present our data to users, while also reducing the learning curve. To overcome these challenges, we conducted multiple A/B testing sessions to balance the pros and cons.

BRAINSTORMING

First, we conducted a brainstorming session and roughly sketched out our ideas. The principle of this session was the ideas must be based on user goals.

After considering their feasibility and discarding some of them, we narrowed it down to four ideas. We then made a medium-fidelity design for each idea and delivered them to users for feedback.

IDEA SYNTHESIS

After organizing user feedback, we analyzed the pros and cons of each idea. Since each idea tackles different needs, we decided to extract the outstanding part of each idea and combine them, as to create a comprehensive visualization. Based on the user goals we established earlier, we selected human glyph, line chart, and chord chart as our core sections.

CHALLENGE - LAYOUT

The layout of our design elements was a tough challenge. We had two positioning options: side-by-side or top-to-bottom. As we were aware of the various pros and cons, we were faced with a difficult decision.

Thus, we conducted an A/B testing session with eight participants. The results reveal that participants take longer to finish tasks in the side-by-side view at first, as they are not familiar to this style of layout. However, with a little bit more practice, participants felt more confident in the side-by-side view, and the result became similar or even better than the top-to-bottom view. Furthermore, participants' feedback revealed a preference to the side-by-side view. This was due to the constant scrolling of the top-to-bottom layout that proved to be irritating and tedious. Thus, we decided to go with the side-by-side layout.

CHALLENGE - CHORD CHART?

During our A/B testing session, many participants mentioned issues they were having understanding the chord chart, as it created a visual cluster for them. This ineffectiveness of the chord chart was not an issue we were excepting.

So...

Is there any other replacement for chord chart to represent the percentage of clothing combinations?

After conducting some research, we found that matrix is a very powerful visualization for the comparison of two items. It caters to our use of combinations, while it was clear to understand. Therefore, we changed our chord chart to matrix and collected more feedback from several users. This revealed to be the right decision based on user feedback.

INTERACTION DESIGN

When designing an information visualization, the interactions are as important as the representations, as it should serve the users to achieve their goals. To make every interaction meaningful, we built off from the user goals that we identified earlier.

Identifying the trends over time

Interaction 1

By dragging the time bar, users can see the major style of cities of a certain period.

Interaction 2

Clicking a city (human glyph), the line chart will show the changes of current attribute over years.

Interaction 3

Using the dropdown menu, users can choose specific attribute and see the changes of the selected attribute over time.

Interaction 4

Clicking another city will add another line to the line chart to represent this city, allowing comparisons.

Interaction 5

Take the attribute “shirt”, for example. Hovering over a certain point will show the percentage of how many people are wearing shirts in this city during this time period. It will also reveal the cities that have the maximum percentage and the minimum percentage.

Identifying the similarities and differences of cities

Interaction 1

After clicking a city, the human glyph view will change. The opacity of the glyph correlates to how similar it is to the selected city. The higher the opacity is, the closer it is to the selected city. Clicking the city again will deselect it.

Interaction 2

Hovering over a city will show the details of this city, such as clothing category, clothing pattern, and major color.

Identifying popular styles of clothing combinations

Interaction 1

Similarly to the interactions of the line chart, clicking a city will show the matrix of this city. The color represents the most popular color of this combination, and the opacity represents the percentage people wearing something like this in this area.

Interaction 2

Clicking another city to add another matrix. When two cities have a similar clothing combination, it will be highlighted when hovering over it.

Interaction 3

Hovering over the square of the matrix will show the detailed explanation of this clothing combination.

Interaction 4

Clicking a square of the matrix will reveal the example pictures.

FINAL PROTOTYPE

EVALUATION

Before delivering our final prototype to users for feedback, we established several metrics to help us evaluate. We then invited several students who are interested in fashion to try out V.style. The results show that our design is very interesting and visually appealing. However, the learnability can still be improved.

EVALUATION METRICS

Since information visualization system is different from traditional apps, our evaluation focus has bee changed to the following aspects. They are:

Learnability - 4.2 / 5.0

Our visualization is relatively new for most users. Thus, learnability has always been our big concern. We would like to know if users can get familiar with our tool very quickly. The results revealed that the learnability still need to be improved. Although we provided an instruction image in the very beginning, users still feel overwhelmed. We might implement a step-by-step storytelling instruction to tackle it.

Efficiency - 4.6 / 5.0

This project is goal-driven. Therefore, accomplishing tasks efficiently is an important metric to evaluate our design. The results revealed that users can quickly finish tasks after getting familiar with how it works.

Insightfulness - 4.6 / 5.0

The ultimate purpose of information visualization is to discover insights. We hope users can discover some interesting findings. Here are some insights that our users found:

  • Everybody loves black and white shirts or T-shirts, no matter where they come from. One world, one "style".
  • The famous fashionable cities like New York and Milan, they share the same styles.
  • Proximity has influences on fashion styles. More closer, more similar.

Satisfaction 4.9 / 5.0

One of our primary goals for this project is to provide joy. We hope it's also a fun tool to use since fashion is also an interesting topic. And it turned out EVERYBODY LOVES IT! They love those cute little human glyphs with different clothing. They are curious about fashion style in other cities. The positive feedback makes us feel happy.

REFLECTION

This project is one of my favorite projects. I’m so proud of what we have accomplished. Information visualization was a new territory for us. An innovate way to present data means more efforts for users to learn. However, an appropriate visualization can help users discover in-depth findings, which is something that simple bar charts cannot achieve. To design an appropriate visualization, we have to be user-driven, focusing on their goals.

During the design phase, we have encountered so many tough challenges that we have to make a choice. Some of them are choices between achievable functions and visual effects, and some are choices among different ways to present data. There’s always a trade-off. What we can do is to balance these trade-offs and think about one question: are you just trying to make it visually amazing? or really trying to design for your users?
