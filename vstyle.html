OVERVIEW

WHAT IS V.STYLE?

V.style is a website that helps users gain insight about various fashion styles across cities and time. The website accomplishes this by visualizing relevant information. The site includes the following views:

  • Glyph view – presents each city by human glyph with different clothing and accessories.
  • Line chart view – displays detailed information of different cities over various years.
  • Matrix view – displays popular style combinations of multiple cities.
  • Similarity view – presents multiple style similarities of different cities.

USER GOALS

Based on the understanding of our dataset and users, we established two outlined goals with several specific sub-goals that our design should help users achieve:

Understanding how clothing styles changes over time and varies around the world

  • Identifying trends over time - Fashion changes quick and constantly. Identifying those changes over time may help users understand how and why these changes occur.
  • Identifying similarities and differences of cities - Every culture has specific influences on the population; this also applies to each city. How people are dressing in a city represents the location and the personality of the area, as well.

Discovering inspirations for dressing up

  • Identifying popular styles of clothing combinations -  Besides the culture exploration of our design, a more practical usage of could assist users and inspire how they match clothing styles.

CHALLENGE - LAYOUT

The layout of our design elements was a tough challenge. We had two positioning options: side-by-side or top-to-bottom. As we were aware of the various pros and cons, we were faced with a difficult decision.

Thus, we conducted an A/B testing session with four users. The results reveal that users take longer to finish tasks in the side-by-side view, as they are not familiar to this style of layout. However, user feedback revealed a preference to the side-by-side view. This was the due to the constant scrolling of the top-to-bottom layout, that proved to be irritating and tedious. Furthermore, with a little bit more practice, users felt more confident in the side-by-side view. Thus, we decided to go with the side-by-side layout.

CHALLENGE - CHORD CHART OR MATRIX?

During our A/B testing session, some users mentioned issues they were having understanding the chord chart, as it created a visual cluster for them. This ineffectiveness of the chord chart was not an issue we were excepting.

So, is there any other replacement for chord chart to represent the percentage of clothing combinations?

After conducting some research, we found that matrix is a very powerful visualization for the comparison of two items. It caters to our use of combinations, while it was clear to understand. Therefore, we changed our chord chart to matrix and collected more feedback from several users. This revealed to be the right decision, based on user feedback.

From the initial low-fi wireframes to final hi-fi prototype, we conducted 3 heuristic evaluation sessions and 20 usability testing sessions in total. The results proved that our final design is usable, efficient, and able to fulfil users’ needs.

REFLECTION

Designing for users is not an easy job to do, especially when you need to consider about business as well, it becomes even harder. There are a lot of trade-offs you need to deal with. For example, during the design process, some restaurants owners would like to give away free drinks or sides instead of actual money, however, most users prefer to get the actual money rebate. Eventually, we decided to give restaurant owners the freedom to set up the discount levels, but it has to be money related. By this way, we help make sure the restaurant can still get profit meanwhile it doesn’t sacrifice users’ interests.

It leads me to think about the relationship between business and user objectives. In this case, as a relatively new service provider, to get into the market and become competitive, FoodEx can’t always think about its stakeholders and restaurant owners. It has to start focusing on users. Putting the users’ needs first is how we attract more users, and eventually benefit our stakeholders and restaurants. As Andrew Kucheriavy, the CEO & founder of Intechnic, said: you can’t reach your business objectives at the expense of those of your users.
